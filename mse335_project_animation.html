Why fast charging can shift graphite from intercalation to lithium plating

This single-page animation illustrates a reduced-order single-particle model idea: lithium arrives at the graphite surface, diffuses inward, and may plate if the surface becomes too crowded under high current, low temperature, or slow diffusion.

The equation story in plain language

This section connects the animation to the reduced-order single-particle model used in the project.

1. Diffusion inside a spherical graphite particle
∂cₛ/∂t = Dₛ · (1/r²) ∂/∂r (r² ∂cₛ/∂r)

Larger Dₛ or smaller Rₚ lets lithium spread inward faster, lowering surface buildup.
2. Applied current becomes surface flux
Nₛ ∝ iapp / F

Increasing C-rate means lithium arrives faster at the particle surface.
3. Diffusion time scale
τdiff = Rₚ² / Dₛ

When the charge time is shorter than this diffusion time scale, concentration gradients become large.
4. Simplified plating rule
plating risk ↑ when cₛ,surf → cₛ,max and flux remains high

In the visualization, risk is based on C-rate, temperature-corrected diffusivity, radius, and initial SOC.
Good project takeaway: intercalation is thermodynamically desired, but kinetics can locally favor lithium metal deposition if lithium arrives faster than graphite can absorb and diffuse it.
